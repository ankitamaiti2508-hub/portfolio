The Problem

The Electronics category on Tata Neu was underperforming relative to its traffic share. Users were browsing but not buying — the category had [X]% higher drop-off at the product detail page (PDP) than the platform average, and cart abandonment was [X]pp above benchmark.

Electronics is inherently a considered purchase — long research cycles, high comparison behaviour, trust-sensitive. The challenge was to reduce friction without oversimplifying a category where users want information, not just speed.

Key Insight

[X]% of PDP visitors did not add to cart. User interviews revealed three recurring themes: inability to compare specs, EMI options buried, and trust signals (warranty, returns) not visible.

The Approach

Building the Funnel Map

Pulled session-level data across [X] weeks. Mapped the full funnel: Homepage → Category page → PDP → Add to Cart → Checkout → Payment → Confirm. Identified the PDP→Cart step as the primary leakage point — [X]% of PDP visitors did not add to cart.

Qualitative Diagnosis

Ran user interviews and session replay analysis to understand why. Three recurring themes: (a) users couldn't easily compare specs across products, (b) EMI/financing options weren't surfaced early enough, (c) product return and warranty information was buried.

Prioritized Interventions

Ranked interventions by effort vs. impact and ran them in sequence: (1) Sticky spec comparison bar on PDP, (2) EMI calculator above the fold, (3) Trust badges (warranty, returns, NeuCoins) on PDP. Ran each as a controlled test.

Key Decisions & Tradeoffs

Sequential tests over a single big release

Isolated signal from each change. Harder to ship, but cleaner learnings that could be replicated in other categories (Jewellery, Appliances).

Prioritised PDP over checkout

Checkout was already well-optimised. The real drop was earlier in the funnel — fixing the wrong stage would have had minimal impact.

EMI above the fold — contentious internally

Some stakeholders worried it made the product look expensive. Data showed it actually increased conversion for high-AOV products — users appreciated transparency.

Sticky spec comparison bar

Required engineering effort to persist across scroll. Alternative was a modal — rejected because it interrupted the browse flow and had lower engagement in testing.

What I'd Do Differently
Retrospective

I'd instrument the comparison feature more granularly from day one — we knew users were using it but didn't track which spec rows drove the most engagement. That data would have informed future catalogue requirements for electronics listings.
