Context

Tata Neu was an early-stage super-app competing in a market where Flipkart and Amazon had already built deep user trust and habitual buying behaviour. Electronics was our highest GMV category — roughly 60–65% of total platform GMV — which made it both our biggest opportunity and our most exposed battleground against established players.

FY23–24 data told a clear story: traffic and new user acquisition were working, but we had two structural gaps. Conversion wasn't keeping pace with traffic growth, and our repeat rate was around 23% — well below the 40–50% that mature platforms were seeing. Users were coming in, browsing electronics, and buying elsewhere — on platforms they'd trusted for years.

The Mandate

Going into FY24–25, I owned the Electronics funnel roadmap — with a mandate to improve conversion, grow new user transactions, and start building a repeat purchase habit.

The Diagnosis

I started by mapping the full funnel and measuring drop-off at each step. PDP-to-cart had the biggest leakage. But I didn't stop there — I segmented PDP exits by price band.

A clear pattern emerged: higher-priced SKUs were converting significantly worse than lower-priced ones. That ruled out UX friction as the primary cause — if it were a UX problem, you'd see uniform drop-off across price bands. The problem was specific to high-AOV items.

The Key Insight

On Flipkart and Amazon, effective price — after bank offers, cashback, and EMI — was prominently surfaced on the PDP. On Neu, users had to work to figure out what they'd actually pay. For a new platform without established trust, that's a fatal gap.

Electronics buyers are highly price-sensitive and already predisposed to trust incumbents. If they can't quickly see they're getting a fair deal, they leave. The diagnosis was clear: this wasn't a UX problem or a product quality problem. It was a pricing perception problem.

The Roadmap Approach

Once the diagnosis was clear, I structured the roadmap in two layers — quick wins first to show early momentum, then longer bets requiring more engineering and cross-functional buy-in. Running them sequentially meant each test was clean — we could attribute impact to the specific intervention rather than have signals bleed into each other.

01
Best Deals Widget
Pricing perception on the PDP · Quick win

PDP-to-cart conversion was at 21%. Price-band segmentation pointed clearly to pricing perception as the cause. Tata Neu had genuinely good offers — bank discounts, product coupons, exchange value — but they were fragmented. A user on a ₹80,000 laptop PDP had to open each offer individually, figure out what was stackable, mentally calculate the exchange value, and arrive at an effective price themselves. That's too much cognitive load at a moment when they're already uncertain about a high-value purchase on a new platform.

The Best Deals widget consolidated all stackable offers a user was eligible for — product offers, coupons, bank discounts — and showed the final price they'd actually pay, both with and without exchange. Users could select their exchange product, select their bank offer, and see the exact final price in real time. No mental math, no uncertainty.

Design Decision 1

Deliberately excluded combo offers — those require adding a second product, which is a different decision entirely. Mixing them in would have complicated the widget and diluted its core purpose: show the best price on this product, right now.

Design Decision 2

Set a configurable threshold — the widget only shows when the effective price after offers is less than 90% of MRP. A widget that shows up with a ₹50 saving on a ₹50,000 product doesn't just fail to help — it actively undermines trust. Every time the widget appears, it needed to be genuinely valuable.

We ran it as an A/B test. Within 2 weeks, PDP-to-cart conversion moved from 21% to 28%.

02
Accessories Recommendation & Extended Warranty
AOV improvement at cart stage

After solving the pricing perception problem, I turned to AOV. Cart composition data showed that most electronics orders were single-item — users were buying the primary product but not picking up accessories. The opportunity was clear: if someone is buying a phone, they likely need a screen protector or a case — we just weren't surfacing it effectively.

I worked with the personalization team to build a compatibility-aware recommendation algorithm. The core insight: accessory recommendations only work if they're relevant — a generic "you might also like" carousel is noise. So we built feature-based compatibility matching: for each primary product, we identified which accessory categories needed a compatibility check and which features had to match.

Key Design Decision

Differentiated the logic by accessory type rather than applying a blanket rule. A screen protector for a phone requires model-series matching (S23 protector won't fit S21). A power bank recommendation doesn't — compatibility is near-universal. This distinction made recommendations genuinely useful rather than generic.

These recommendations appeared on the PDP — before the user added to cart — so the accessory was part of the purchase decision, not an afterthought. Extended Warranty was handled separately, surfaced as a standalone recommendation at the right moment. It's a fundamentally different proposition — a protection plan, not a product — so bundling it with accessories would have muddied the message.

03
Platform Offers for New User Transactors
Breaking the first-purchase trust barrier · Infrastructure build

First-time transactors needed more motivation to complete their initial purchase. Tata Neu gives NeuCoins on every transaction, but NeuCoins benefit a future transaction — they don't reduce what you pay today. For a hesitant new user making a high-value electronics purchase on an unfamiliar platform, that's insufficient motivation.

When we tried to execute a platform-wide new user offer, we hit a fundamental infrastructure gap: every brand on Tata Neu ran its own offer engine in isolation with no cross-brand communication. A user could redeem a new user offer on Croma and redeem it again on BigBasket — because neither engine knew what the other had done.

The Solution

Tata Digital's central offer engine as the backbone with a Segment-as-a-Service layer to maintain real-time user state. When a user registers, a Golden Records database entry triggers a new user event that updates their segment. When they apply the coupon at any brand, the central validate API checks their segment in real time. On redemption, a central redemption API immediately updates the segment — offer can't be claimed again anywhere on the platform.

On the Croma journey, I integrated platform coupons at three touchpoints — PLP, PDP, and cart — but only for eligible users (new and anonymous). The coupon card surfaces savings value, offer description, and coupon code. In the Best Deals widget, this coupon discount is factored in alongside bank offers and exchange value to show the true final price.

Two edge cases I solved explicitly:

1
Concurrent application — a user could theoretically apply the coupon simultaneously from two devices in two brand journeys. Solution: 10-minute block on the coupon the moment it's applied — can't be used anywhere on the platform during that window.
2
Channel integrity — this was a Tata Neu exclusive offer; applying the code on Croma.com directly should be rejected. Solution: channel ID check in the validation flow, along with required fraud risk management checks.

04
NeuPass Loyalty Tier Benefits
Building a repeat purchase habit

We had a loyalty programme — NeuPass — with four tiers determined by a customer's transaction history. Higher tiers earned better NeuCoin percentages and exclusive offers. But in the Electronics journey, none of this was visible. Users didn't know what tier they were on, what they were earning, or what exclusive offers they were eligible for. The loyalty programme existed, but it wasn't doing any work inside the purchase journey.

The fix had two parts:

First, I surfaced tier identity and NeuCoin earnings directly in the Electronics journey — so a user could see their current tier and exactly how many NeuCoins they'd earn on a given order before checkout. This made the loyalty programme feel tangible and immediate, not abstract.

Second, I integrated exclusive tier offers into the journey at three touchpoints — PLP, PDP, and cart. The technical mechanism worked through C# validation: when a user lands in the journey, their customer hash identifies their tier segment, surfacing the offers they're exclusively eligible for.

Design Decision

Exclusive tier coupons were visually differentiated — highlighted with a special NeuPass design on the coupon card, so users immediately understood this wasn't a generic offer, it was something they'd earned. Making exclusivity feel real was critical to making loyalty tangible.

Increase in user retention in Electronics category
Key Decisions & Tradeoffs

Sequential tests over a single big release

Running interventions one at a time meant clean attribution — we knew exactly which change drove which result. Harder to ship, but produced defensible learnings we could replicate across other categories (Jewellery, Appliances).

Price perception before UX

The instinct would have been to redesign the PDP. The data showed it was a pricing confidence problem, not a design problem. Solving the wrong problem first would have wasted 2–3 months of engineering.

90% MRP threshold on Best Deals widget

A widget showing a trivial saving actively hurts trust on a platform users don't yet trust. The threshold ensured the widget only appeared when it was genuinely valuable — protecting both conversion and brand credibility.

New user offer funded from acquisition budget

Not from category margin — this was critical for stakeholder alignment. The offer was framed as an acquisition cost, not a discount, which changed how commercial leadership evaluated its ROI.

Accessories on PDP, not cart

Surfacing accessories before add-to-cart made them part of the purchase decision. At cart, they feel like an upsell. On PDP, they feel like useful context. Same product, very different user psychology.

Extended Warranty surfaced separately

Bundling it with accessories would have muddied the message. A protection plan is a different proposition — it needed its own moment in the journey to be taken seriously.

What I'd Do Differently
Retrospective

I'd instrument the Best Deals widget more granularly from day one — we knew it was driving conversion but didn't track which specific offer type (bank discount vs. exchange vs. coupon) was the primary driver. That data would have informed where to invest further — whether to expand bank partnerships, improve the exchange programme, or prioritize coupon variety. I'd also push to bring the widget to the PLP earlier — we planned it but it didn't make the roadmap before I moved on.
