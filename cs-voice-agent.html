The Problem

In our eB2B retail distribution model, daily ordering heavily depends on salesmen visiting retailers physically. However, there are two major operational gaps: salesman absenteeism and vacant sales routes. Together, these were leading to significant missed retailer engagement and lost orders on a daily basis.

There are ways of recovery — such as the tele-sell team, RSU vehicles, and self-ordering app (Coke Buddy) — but these are only able to recover 30% of the lost revenue. The tele-sell team has limited calling bandwidth and hence is not able to cover all outlets which are at risk of lost sales.

Hypothesis

Can we create an always-available, scalable, low-cost sales recovery layer that recovers otherwise lost revenue while maintaining acceptable unit economics?

My Role
  • Built the business case around the annual revenue leakage caused by field sales gaps, and how much of it can be recovered via an Agentic AI voice solution
  • Analysed tele-sell team call recordings to design the end-to-end call flow
  • Developed an LLM-powered voice agent by partnering with vendors for automated retailer order taking
  • Designed end-to-end agentic workflows powered by retailer context retrieval and product scheme look-up to enable conversational selling, objection-handling, and automated call-backs
  • Ran 3 competitive vendor pilots with definitive goals in a phased manner with a structured KPI framework; analysed call transcripts and no-order reasons to iteratively refine prompt strategies, agent decision trees, and escalation flows
  • Improved conversion rate 58% (6.0% → 9.5%) and AOV by 11%; developed unit economics projecting 30% annualised net revenue recovery
High-Level Architecture
Approach Overview

I structured the pilots progressively. I first conducted a small pilot with one vendor to understand the problem and establish a baseline. After establishing a baseline, I conducted a second pilot with the same vendor — this time incorporating learnings from the first pilot to improve the workflow and validate whether it led to improved results. Once I had a richer understanding of the workflow from the second pilot, I went ahead with a third pilot evaluating additional vendors. In the fourth pilot, I moved into a structured optimization phase focused on conversion, AOV, and unit economics — an ongoing phase — with the selected vendor.

The Pilots

Stage 1 — Baseline Validation

I started with a vendor because my first goal was to establish a baseline. I designed a simple order-taking workflow based on analysis of existing tele-sell conversations and tested whether retailers would:

  • engage with an AI voice agent
  • trust the interaction
  • and place orders through the channel

The first pilot helped us identify major friction points:

  • retailer skepticism
  • stock already present
  • callback requests
  • and preference for ordering through salesmen

At this stage, the biggest outcome wasn't conversion. It was learning.

Stage 2 — Workflow Optimization

After identifying the friction points in the first pilot, I designed an improved workflow that:

  • removed unnatural phrases
  • introduced callback flows
  • added distributor context
  • incorporated past order history
  • and improved FAQ handling

This led to an improvement in the funnel, particularly engaged-to-order conversion, which increased from 6.5% to 9%. More importantly, we generated a much richer understanding of retailer behaviour and conversation failure points.

Stage 3 — Benchmark Alternative Vendors

Once I had a clearer picture of what a successful AI sales conversation required, I engaged additional vendors. By this stage, I had a much more mature evaluation framework. I knew we needed capabilities such as:

  • skepticism handling
  • objection handling
  • callback management
  • contextual personalization
  • and transcript visibility

Instead of evaluating generic AI capabilities, I evaluated each vendor against the specific retailer behaviours we had observed in earlier pilots.

Stage 4 — Deep-Dive with the Best Performer

One vendor emerged as the strongest performer across conversion, conversation quality, and workflow flexibility. Rather than immediately scaling, I treated that as another learning phase. I designed a structured experimentation roadmap around:

  • opening strategies
  • objection handling
  • basket expansion
  • and reducing non-productive call time

The goal shifted from validating feasibility to optimising conversion, AOV, and cost-to-serve.

Key Decisions & Tradeoffs

Voice-first over app-first

Retailers in coverage gaps often had low smartphone literacy; voice reduced friction substantially. An app would have required onboarding, downloads, and data literacy.

Built fallback to human agent

Maintaining trust in the AI required a clear escape hatch. This also helped us collect failure cases for model improvement — invaluable early signal.

Constrained SKU catalogue intentionally

Started with top 80% revenue SKUs to reduce ambiguity in voice recognition. Full catalogue would have increased disambiguation errors by an estimated [X]x.

Phased rollout over big-bang

Starting with [X] pilot outlets gave us real error rates and retailer feedback before scaling. Slower to start, but prevented a poor experience at scale.

What I'd Do Differently
Retrospective

I'd involve retailers earlier in the conversation flow design — we iterated more than expected on SKU disambiguation because we underestimated how locally-named products are in practice ("Thums Up" vs. "Cola 200ml"). Earlier co-design sessions would have reduced this.
