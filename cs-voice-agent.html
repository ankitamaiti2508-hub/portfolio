The Problem

In our eB2B retail distribution model, daily ordering heavily depends on salesmen visiting retailers physically. However, there are two major operational gaps: salesman absenteeism and vacant sales routes. Together, these were leading to significant missed retailer engagement and lost orders on a daily basis.

There are ways of recovery — such as the tele-sell team, RSU vehicles, and self-ordering app (Coke Buddy) — but these are only able to recover 30% of the lost revenue. The tele-sell team has limited calling bandwidth and hence is not able to cover all outlets which are at risk of lost sales.

Hypothesis

Can we create an always-available, scalable, low-cost sales recovery layer that recovers otherwise lost revenue while maintaining acceptable unit economics?

My Role
  • Built the business case around the annual revenue leakage caused by field sales gaps, and how much of it can be recovered via an Agentic AI voice solution
  • Analysed tele-sell team call recordings to design the end-to-end call flow
  • Developed an LLM-powered voice agent by partnering with vendors for automated retailer order taking
  • Designed end-to-end agentic workflows powered by retailer context retrieval and product scheme look-up to enable conversational selling, objection-handling, and automated call-backs
  • Ran 3 competitive vendor pilots with definitive goals in a phased manner with a structured KPI framework; analysed call transcripts and no-order reasons to iteratively refine prompt strategies, agent decision trees, and escalation flows
  • Improved conversion rate 58% (6.0% → 9.5%) and AOV by 11%; developed unit economics projecting 30% annualised net revenue recovery
High-Level Architecture
Approach Overview

I structured the pilots progressively. I first conducted a small pilot with one vendor to understand the problem and establish a baseline. After establishing a baseline, I conducted a second pilot with the same vendor — this time incorporating learnings from the first pilot to improve the workflow and validate whether it led to improved results. Once I had a richer understanding of the workflow from the second pilot, I went ahead with a third pilot evaluating additional vendors. In the fourth pilot, I moved into a structured optimization phase focused on conversion, AOV, and unit economics — an ongoing phase — with the selected vendor.

The Pilots

Stage 1 — Baseline Validation

I started with a vendor because my first goal was to establish a baseline. I designed a simple order-taking workflow based on analysis of existing tele-sell conversations and tested whether retailers would:

  • engage with an AI voice agent
  • trust the interaction
  • and place orders through the channel

The first pilot helped us identify major friction points:

  • retailer skepticism
  • stock already present
  • callback requests
  • and preference for ordering through salesmen

At this stage, the biggest outcome wasn't conversion. It was learning.

Stage 2 — Workflow Optimization

After identifying the friction points in the first pilot, I designed an improved workflow that:

  • removed unnatural phrases
  • introduced callback flows
  • added distributor context
  • incorporated past order history
  • and improved FAQ handling

This led to an improvement in the funnel, particularly engaged-to-order conversion, which increased from 6.5% to 9%. More importantly, we generated a much richer understanding of retailer behaviour and conversation failure points.

Stage 3 — Benchmark Alternative Vendors

Once I had a clearer picture of what a successful AI sales conversation required, I engaged additional vendors. By this stage, I had a much more mature evaluation framework. I knew we needed capabilities such as:

  • skepticism handling
  • objection handling
  • callback management
  • contextual personalization
  • and transcript visibility

Instead of evaluating generic AI capabilities, I evaluated each vendor against the specific retailer behaviours we had observed in earlier pilots.

Stage 4 — Deep-Dive with the Best Performer

One vendor emerged as the strongest performer across conversion, conversation quality, and workflow flexibility. Rather than immediately scaling, I treated that as another learning phase. I designed a structured experimentation roadmap around:

  • opening strategies
  • objection handling
  • basket expansion
  • and reducing non-productive call time

The goal shifted from validating feasibility to optimising conversion, AOV, and cost-to-serve.

The Big Idea — Why "Multi-Agent"?

A single AI agent trying to do everything — verify the retailer, pitch products, handle complaints, manage objections, close the call — becomes very hard to build, test, and improve. Each task requires different context, different logic, and different failure modes.

The multi-agent approach breaks the call into specialist agents, each owning one job. The Orchestrator sits above them all and decides which agent should be active at any moment — like a call center supervisor routing between specialists.

Layer 1 — The Orchestrator

Think of this as the "brain" of the system. It:

  • Maintains the state of the conversation at all times — what stage are we at, what has been said, what has been agreed
  • Listens to every turn of the conversation and decides: should I stay in the current agent, or hand off to another?
  • Passes context between agents — so when Pitch Agent takes over from Intro Agent, it already knows the retailer's name, tone, and what was said
Key Principle

The Orchestrator never speaks to the retailer directly. It only routes.

Layer 2 — The Linear Flow (a normal call)

Agent 1 — Verification

The call connects. The first thing the system does is confirm it's speaking to the right store. It asks: "Am I speaking with Store X?" It checks the response against the Retailer DB — store name, owner name, phone number. Two outcomes: verified (proceed) or wrong store (hand off to Wrong Store interrupt agent, close gracefully).

Agent 2 — Intro

Once verified, the Intro Agent takes over. It explains why it's calling — "Your salesman is absent today, so I've called to take your order." Then it warms up the conversation — asks how business is, how sales have been. This does two things: builds rapport so the retailer doesn't feel like they're talking to a cold bot, and reads the retailer's tone. Is the retailer engaged? Busy? Irritated? The Orchestrator picks up these signals and decides whether to proceed to pitch, or hand off to an interrupt agent.

Agent 3 — Pitch

This is the core of the call. The Pitch Agent does two things before opening its mouth:

  • Pulls the retailer's past order history to know what they typically buy — and leads with those SKUs because that's what the retailer already wants
  • Checks active schemes and offers and surfaces the most attractive ones relevant to this retailer's category

Then it asks about stock. The retailer's answer determines which path the call takes next.

Agent 4A / 4B — Stock Handling (where it gets intelligent)

If the retailer says "I have stock" — the system doesn't give up. Agent 4A (Stock Agent) asks specifically what they have, cross-references it against their usual order basket, and identifies what's missing. "I see you usually order Thums Up 2L — are you running low on that?" This is targeted, not generic.

If the retailer says they have everything — Agent 4B (AOV Agent) pivots to low-AOV products: small packs, impulse SKUs, new launches. It also tries alternate pitch angles — scheme urgency ("this offer ends Friday"), social proof ("the store next to you just picked up 20 cases"). If nothing works, it moves gracefully toward close rather than pushing.

Agent 5 — Order Confirmation

Once the basket is built, the Order Agent reads it back to the retailer — every SKU, every quantity, every scheme applied. Gets explicit confirmation. Then places the order in the Order Management System and shares an order reference number. This step creates a paper trail and prevents disputes.

Agent 6 — Close

The Close Agent thanks the retailer, mentions expected delivery, and ends the call warmly. It also logs the full call outcome to the CRM — what was ordered, what schemes were applied, any flags raised during the call.

Layer 3 — The Interrupt Agents (when the call goes sideways)

These agents can fire at any point in the conversation. The Orchestrator is always listening for trigger signals.

Wrong Store Agent

Fires during verification if the person who picks up isn't the right retailer. Apologises, closes politely, logs as wrong number.

Callback Agent

Fires if the retailer says they're busy at any point. Doesn't push. Asks when would be a good time, schedules a callback, closes the call. The system will then auto-dial at the scheduled time.

Service Request Agent

Fires if the retailer raises a complaint — cooler not working, delivery didn't arrive, damaged goods. Acknowledges empathetically first, looks up the Service FAQs DB, raises a service ticket — and critically returns to the pitch flow after handling the complaint. The complaint doesn't end the call.

Objection Handler Agent

Fires during the pitch if the retailer pushes back. "Your competitor is cheaper." "I don't need that SKU." "Last time quality was bad." Has pre-trained responses for common objections, can surface a relevant scheme to counter a price objection, and hands back to the Pitch Agent once resolved.

Skepticism Handler Agent

Fires when a retailer questions the legitimacy of the call — "Who are you? Why is a bot calling me?" Rather than explaining itself abstractly, it builds trust by surfacing specific account details the retailer recognises: their distributor name, last order date, and SKUs ordered. A retailer who hears their own order history reflected back immediately understands this is a legitimate call. Once trust is established, the Orchestrator returns to wherever the conversation was.

Layer 4 — The Context Databases

All agents draw from the same three databases — they don't re-fetch independently. The Orchestrator passes the relevant context to whichever agent is active.

Retailer Context

Who they are, what they've ordered before, how often, their category, credit terms.

Business Context

What SKUs exist, what the current pricing is, what schemes are active.

Operational Context

Inventory levels, delivery timelines, distributor mapping, service FAQs, policies.

What makes this "agentic"

The system isn't following a rigid script. At each turn, the Orchestrator interprets what the retailer said, decides what it means, and chooses the right next action — staying in the current agent, handing off to a specialist, or firing an interrupt. It's goal-directed (place the order) but adaptive (handles anything the retailer throws at it).

Key Decisions & Tradeoffs

Voice-first over app-first

Retailers in coverage gaps often had low smartphone literacy; voice reduced friction substantially. An app would have required onboarding, downloads, and data literacy.

Built fallback to human agent

Maintaining trust in the AI required a clear escape hatch. This also helped us collect failure cases for model improvement — invaluable early signal.

Constrained SKU catalogue intentionally

Started with top 80% revenue SKUs to reduce ambiguity in voice recognition. Full catalogue would have increased disambiguation errors by an estimated [X]x.

Phased rollout over big-bang

Starting with [X] pilot outlets gave us real error rates and retailer feedback before scaling. Slower to start, but prevented a poor experience at scale.

What I'd Do Differently
Retrospective

I'd involve retailers earlier in the conversation flow design — we iterated more than expected on SKU disambiguation because we underestimated how locally-named products are in practice ("Thums Up" vs. "Cola 200ml"). Earlier co-design sessions would have reduced this.
