The Problem

HCCB's field sales model depends on salespeople physically visiting retail outlets to take orders. But coverage is finite — a salesperson can only visit so many outlets per day, and outlets in low-density or remote areas often go unserved for days at a stretch.

The result: lost orders, reduced retailer loyalty, and revenue leakage that was structurally hard to address through headcount alone.

The Approach

Validating the Hypothesis

Before committing to an AI solution, I ran structured interviews with field sales officers and retailers to confirm that voice was a viable channel — and that the drop-off in coverage was actually causing lost orders, not just delayed ones. Data confirmed [X]% of uncovered outlets placed zero orders in off-visit weeks.

Designing the Agent

The core challenge was designing a voice interaction that felt natural to a kirana retailer — not a tech-savvy user. I mapped the full conversation flow: greeting and authentication, catalogue browsing by category or SKU name, quantity confirmation, order summary, and final submit.

We built fallback logic for misheard SKU names (common brand names vs. formal SKU codes), multi-language support (Hindi + regional languages), and a human handoff trigger when confidence scores dropped below threshold.

Integration and Rollout

Worked with engineering to integrate the LLM backbone with HCCB's existing order management system. Ran a phased rollout starting with [X] pilot outlets, measuring conversion, order value, and error rates before scaling.

Key Decisions & Tradeoffs

Voice-first over app-first

Retailers in coverage gaps often had low smartphone literacy; voice reduced friction substantially. An app would have required onboarding, downloads, and data literacy.

Built fallback to human agent

Maintaining trust in the AI required a clear escape hatch. This also helped us collect failure cases for model improvement — invaluable early signal.

Constrained SKU catalogue intentionally

Started with top 80% revenue SKUs to reduce ambiguity in voice recognition. Full catalogue would have increased disambiguation errors by an estimated [X]x.

Phased rollout over big-bang

Starting with [X] pilot outlets gave us real error rates and retailer feedback before scaling. Slower to start, but prevented a poor experience at scale.

What I'd Do Differently
Retrospective

I'd involve retailers earlier in the conversation flow design — we iterated more than expected on SKU disambiguation because we underestimated how locally-named products are in practice ("Thums Up" vs. "Cola 200ml"). Earlier co-design sessions would have reduced this.
